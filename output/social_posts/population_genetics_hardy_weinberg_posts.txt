TITLE: [OC] Simulating Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium and Genetic Drift in Python

BODY:

I built a Python simulation to explore one of the foundational concepts in population genetics: the Hardy-Weinberg principle.

**ELI5 Version:**
Imagine a population of organisms with two versions of a gene (alleles). The Hardy-Weinberg equation tells us what percentage of the population will have each combination IF nothing is changing the gene frequencies (no natural selection, everyone mates randomly, etc.).

The equation is: p² + 2pq + q² = 1

Where:
- p = frequency of allele A
- q = frequency of allele a (and p + q = 1)
- p² = frequency of AA individuals
- 2pq = frequency of Aa individuals
- q² = frequency of aa individuals

**What I learned:**

1. **Genetic drift is powerful in small populations** - With N=50, allele frequencies swung wildly and sometimes hit fixation (0 or 1). With N=5000, frequencies barely budged from the starting point.

2. **Maximum heterozygosity occurs at p = 0.5** - The "Hardy-Weinberg parabola" shows that genetic diversity (heterozygotes) peaks when both alleles are equally common.

3. **Chi-squared testing** - Used χ² = Σ(O-E)²/E to test whether observed genotype counts match Hardy-Weinberg expectations.

Libraries used: NumPy, SciPy, Matplotlib
