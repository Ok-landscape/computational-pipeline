**Title:** I visualized the Riemann Zeta Function in Python - here's what the non-trivial zeros look like

**Body:**

I've always been fascinated by the Riemann Hypothesis, so I decided to implement and visualize ζ(s) from scratch in Python.

**What is the zeta function?**

For Re(s) > 1, it's defined as:

ζ(s) = ∑(1/nˢ) for n = 1 to ∞

But here's the cool part—Euler discovered it connects directly to prime numbers:

ζ(s) = ∏(1/(1 - p⁻ˢ)) over all primes p

**The Riemann Hypothesis**

When you analytically continue ζ(s) to the complex plane, it has zeros at negative even integers (-2, -4, -6...). These are "trivial."

The million-dollar question: Do ALL non-trivial zeros have Re(s) = 1/2?

**What I implemented:**

1. Complex zeta using Dirichlet series + reflection formula
2. Domain coloring to visualize phase and magnitude
3. 3D surface plot of |ζ(s)|
4. Magnitude along the critical line showing zeros

**Key findings from visualization:**

- ζ(2) = π²/6 ≈ 1.6449 (Basel problem)
- ζ(-1) = -1/12 (yes, that famous sum)
- Non-trivial zeros at t ≈ 14.135, 21.022, 25.011, 30.425...
- All observed zeros lie exactly on Re(s) = 1/2

The domain coloring plot is particularly beautiful—zeros appear as points where all colors converge.
