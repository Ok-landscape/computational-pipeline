**Title:** I implemented Simpson's Rule and finally understand why it's so much better than the trapezoid rule

**Body:**

I've been working through numerical integration methods and wanted to share what clicked for me about Simpson's Rule.

**The basic idea:** Instead of connecting points with straight lines (trapezoid rule), Simpson's Rule fits parabolas through every three consecutive points. Sounds like a small change, but the results are dramatic.

**Why it matters:**
- Trapezoid rule: error scales as O(h²)
- Simpson's rule: error scales as O(h⁴)

This means if you halve your step size:
- Trapezoid error drops by 4×
- Simpson's error drops by 16×

**The formula:**
∫f(x)dx ≈ (h/3)[f₀ + 4f₁ + 2f₂ + 4f₃ + 2f₄ + ... + 4fₙ₋₁ + fₙ]

The weights (1, 4, 2, 4, 2, ..., 4, 1) come from the parabolic approximation.

**Cool discovery:** Simpson's Rule is *exact* for any polynomial of degree 3 or less. I tested it on f(x) = x³ - 2x² + x and got machine-precision accuracy with just 3 points.

**Comparison test on ∫e^(-x²)dx from 0 to 1:**
- n=32: Trapezoid error = 4.94e-06, Simpson error = 1.35e-10
- That's almost 5 orders of magnitude better!

I also implemented an adaptive version that recursively subdivides intervals where error is too high—useful for oscillatory functions.
