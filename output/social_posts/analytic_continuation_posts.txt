Hey r/learnpython! I just created an interactive notebook exploring one of the most powerful techniques in complex analysis: **analytic continuation**.

**What is it?**
Imagine you have a function that only works in a certain region - like the infinite series âˆ‘(1/n^s), which only converges when the real part of s is greater than 1. But what if the function *should* exist outside that region? Analytic continuation is the process of extending it.

**What I learned:**

1. **Power series stepping stones**: You can extend a function by creating overlapping power series centered at different points. Where they overlap, they must agree (Identity Theorem).

2. **The Riemann zeta function**: The famous Î¶(s) = âˆ‘(1/n^s) only converges for Re(s) > 1, but through analytic continuation, we can define it everywhere except s=1. This continuation reveals the "non-trivial zeros" on the critical line Re(s)=1/2, which are at the heart of the Riemann Hypothesis!

3. **Multi-valued functions**: Some functions like log(z) become multi-valued when continued. If you start at z=1 and go around the origin in a circle, you don't come back to the same value - you're off by 2Ï€i! This is called monodromy.

- A color map of |Î¶(s)| showing the zeta function across the complex plane
- The spiral structure of the complex logarithm's branches
- Error analysis comparing different power series approximations
- A phase portrait showing the analytic structure
