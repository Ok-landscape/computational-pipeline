**Title:** I simulated the Logistic Map to visualize how chaos emerges from a simple equation [Python, OC]

**Body:**

**ELI5 version:**

Imagine a population of rabbits. Each generation, the population depends on how many rabbits there were before. With a simple growth model, you'd expect things to stabilize, right?

Wrong.

The logistic map is one equation: x_{n+1} = r·x_n(1-x_n)

When you slowly increase the growth rate r, something wild happens:

1. First, the population settles to a single value (boring)
2. Then it starts bouncing between 2 values
3. Then 4 values, then 8, then 16...
4. Then suddenly: CHAOS. The population never repeats.

**The mind-blowing part:**

The rate at which these period-doublings occur follows a universal constant called the Feigenbaum delta (δ ≈ 4.669). This same number appears in completely different systems - dripping faucets, heart rhythms, even stock markets.

**What I learned:**

- How to compute bifurcation diagrams efficiently in NumPy
- Lyapunov exponents quantify chaos (positive = chaotic, negative = stable)
- Simple deterministic systems can produce unpredictable behavior

The visualization shows the bifurcation diagram, time series for different regimes, and the Lyapunov exponent crossing zero at the onset of chaos.

Code is pure Python with NumPy and Matplotlib.
