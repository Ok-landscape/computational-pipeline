Why do things get stretched into spaghetti near black holes?

It's called geodesic deviation. In flat space, parallel lines stay parallel forever. But spacetime curvature changes that - nearby paths accelerate apart (or together).

The equation: D²ξ/dτ² = R·u·u·ξ

That R is the Riemann curvature tensor - the mathematical heart of gravity. Near a mass M at distance r:
- Radial: +2M/r³ (stretching)
- Transverse: -M/r³ (squishing)

This is literally what LIGO measures to detect gravitational waves.

**Title:** I simulated geodesic deviation (tidal forces) in Schwarzschild spacetime with Python - here's what I learned

**Body:**

Geodesic deviation is one of the most elegant concepts in general relativity. It answers a simple question: if two particles are falling freely side by side, do they stay together?

In flat spacetime, yes. But in curved spacetime, the answer is the **geodesic deviation equation**:

D²ξ/dτ² = R·u·u·ξ

Where ξ is the separation vector between geodesics, u is the 4-velocity, and R is the Riemann curvature tensor.

**What this means physically:**

The Riemann tensor - this abstract 4-index object - directly measures tidal gravitational forces. For a Schwarzschild black hole:

- Radial direction: +2M/r³ (stretching toward the hole)
- Transverse direction: -M/r³ (compression perpendicular to infall)

This is "spaghettification" - you get stretched long and squeezed thin.

**What I built:**

A Python simulation using scipy's odeint to numerically integrate the deviation equations for a radially infalling observer. The visualization shows:

1. Deviation vectors growing/shrinking over proper time
2. The infall trajectory approaching r = 2M
3. A sphere deforming into an ellipsoid
4. Tidal tensor components vs radius (that 1/r³ scaling is brutal)

**Key insight:** This is exactly what LIGO/VIRGO measure. Gravitational waves cause geodesic deviation between their test masses. The detector is literally a geodesic deviation measurement device.
