**Body:**

Hey everyone! I've been diving into differential geometry and wanted to share an interactive notebook I made about the covariant derivative.

**The Problem (ELI5):**
Imagine you're an ant walking on a sphere, carrying an arrow (vector). If you walk in a straight line and try to keep the arrow pointing the "same direction," something weird happens - when you return to your starting point, the arrow has rotated! This is called holonomy, and it happens because the surface is curved.

Regular partial derivatives don't account for this. They only track how vector components change, ignoring that the coordinate axes themselves rotate as you move.

**The Solution:**
The covariant derivative adds a correction term using Christoffel symbols:

nabla_j V^i = partial_j V^i + Gamma^i_jk V^k

These Gamma symbols encode how your coordinate basis rotates from point to point.

**What the notebook covers:**
1. Computing Christoffel symbols for polar and spherical coordinates
2. Implementing covariant derivatives numerically
3. Visualizing the difference between partial and covariant derivatives
4. Parallel transport simulation showing holonomy = 2pi(1 - cos(theta))
5. Verifying that great circles satisfy the geodesic equation

**Key result:** When I transported a vector around a latitude circle at 60 deg from the pole, it rotated by exactly 180 deg - matching the theoretical prediction!

**Why it matters:**
- GPS satellites use this math to account for spacetime curvature
- General relativity is entirely built on covariant derivatives
- Gauge theories in particle physics use the same structure
