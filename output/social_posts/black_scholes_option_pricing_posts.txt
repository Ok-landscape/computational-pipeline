### 5. Reddit (r/learnpython or r/QuantFinance)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**Title:** Implemented the Black-Scholes Option Pricing Model in Python with Greeks and Monte Carlo Validation

**Body:**

I built a complete implementation of the Black-Scholes option pricing model and wanted to share what I learned.

**What is Black-Scholes?**

Published in 1973 by Fischer Black, Myron Scholes, and Robert Merton, this formula lets you calculate the "fair price" of European-style options. It won the Nobel Prize in Economics and is still foundational in quantitative finance.

**The Core Formula**

For a call option: C = S·N(d₁) - K·e^(-rT)·N(d₂)

Where:
- S = current stock price
- K = strike price
- T = time to expiration
- r = risk-free rate
- N() = cumulative normal distribution
- d₁ and d₂ are intermediate calculations involving volatility (σ)

**What I Implemented**

1. **Analytical pricing** for both calls and puts
2. **Greeks** - sensitivities that traders use for hedging:
   - Delta (Δ): How much the option price changes per $1 move in stock
   - Gamma (Γ): How fast Delta changes
   - Theta (Θ): Time decay per day
   - Vega (ν): Sensitivity to volatility
3. **Monte Carlo validation** - simulated 100,000 price paths to verify the formula
4. **Implied volatility solver** using Newton-Raphson iteration

**Key Results**

For a $100 stock with 20% volatility, 1-year expiration, and 5% risk-free rate:
- Call price: $10.45
- Put price: $5.57
- Put-call parity holds perfectly

The Monte Carlo simulation matched the analytical results within $0.01.

**What I Learned**

- The elegance of risk-neutral pricing
- How Greeks provide intuition about option behavior
- Why implied volatility is so important in practice
- The assumptions behind the model (constant volatility, no dividends)
