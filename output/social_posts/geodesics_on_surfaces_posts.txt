Hey everyone! I just finished a computational geometry project and wanted to share what I learned about finding the shortest paths on curved surfaces.

**What are geodesics?**

A geodesic is the generalization of a "straight line" to curved surfaces. It's the shortest path between two points. On Earth, these are the great circles that airplanes follow!

**The Math (simplified):**

Any surface can be described with two coordinates (u, v). The metric tells you how to measure distances:

ds¬≤ = E du¬≤ + 2F du dv + G dv¬≤

Where E, F, G depend on how the surface curves.

To find geodesics, you solve the geodesic equation - a system of second-order differential equations involving Christoffel symbols (Œì‚Å±‚±º‚Çñ). These symbols encode the curvature information.

**What I implemented:**

1. **Sphere geodesics** - These turn out to be great circles, which makes sense! For a sphere with radius R:
   - Metric: ds¬≤ = R¬≤ dŒ∏¬≤ + R¬≤ sin¬≤(Œ∏) dœÜ¬≤
   - The Christoffel symbols tell you how the path curves

2. **Torus geodesics** - Way more interesting! A torus has major radius R and minor radius r. Geodesics can:
   - Go around the outer/inner equators
   - Wind around in complex patterns
   - Potentially cover the surface densely

**Technical approach:**

- Used numpy for array operations
- scipy.integrate.odeint to solve the ODE system
- matplotlib for 3D visualization
- Created mesh surfaces with plot_surface()
- Overlaid geodesic paths in contrasting colors

**Key lessons learned:**

1. Differential geometry isn't just abstract math - it's computable!
2. The geodesic equation is stiff; needed many integration points
3. Christoffel symbols have lots of terms but many are zero by symmetry
4. Initial velocity vector determines the geodesic path completely

**Why this matters:**

- **General Relativity**: Planets/light follow geodesics in curved spacetime
- **Computer Graphics**: Texture mapping and surface parameterization
- **Robotics**: Path planning on configuration spaces (which are manifolds)
- **Optimal Control**: Energy-minimizing trajectories

Computational Differential Geometry: Geodesic Path Finding on Parametric Surfaces

I recently completed a project implementing geodesic computation for curved surfaces, combining differential geometry theory with numerical methods.

**Technical Overview:**

Geodesics are curves that locally minimize arc length on a surface - the generalization of straight lines to curved spaces. Computing them requires:

1. Parameterizing the surface r(u,v) in R¬≥
2. Computing the first fundamental form (metric tensor) with coefficients E, F, G
3. Deriving Christoffel symbols Œì‚Å±‚±º‚Çñ from metric derivatives
4. Solving the geodesic equation (second-order ODE system)
5. Numerical integration using adaptive step-size methods

**Implementation:**

- Python stack: NumPy (vectorization), SciPy (ODE solver), Matplotlib (3D visualization)
- Surfaces tested: sphere and torus with analytical metric tensors
- Method: scipy.integrate.odeint for solving stiff ODEs
- Validation: sphere geodesics converge to great circles (analytical solution)

**Key Insights:**

‚Ä¢ Initial velocity vector completely determines geodesic trajectory
‚Ä¢ Torus geodesics exhibit complex winding behavior depending on starting conditions
‚Ä¢ Christoffel symbol computation benefits from metric symmetries

**Applications:**

This methodology applies to: general relativity simulations (geodesics = free-fall paths), robotics motion planning on configuration manifolds, computer graphics (texture coordinate generation), and optimal control theory.
