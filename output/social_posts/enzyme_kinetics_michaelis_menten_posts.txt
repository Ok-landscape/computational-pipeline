Ever wondered how enzymes work at the molecular level?

The Michaelis-Menten equation is foundational:
v = (Vmax · [S]) / (Km + [S])

Where:
• Vmax = maximum reaction velocity (enzyme saturated)
• Km = substrate concentration at half Vmax

I simulated this with Python - generated synthetic data with noise, then recovered parameters using curve fitting. The classic Lineweaver-Burk plot (1/v vs 1/[S]) gives you a nice linear form, but nonlinear regression is more accurate.

Lower Km means higher affinity - the enzyme "grabs" substrate more efficiently!

Key findings:
• Nonlinear regression outperforms Lineweaver-Burk linearization for parameter estimation
• Low Km → high enzyme-substrate affinity
• At [S] >> Km, reaction becomes zero-order (saturated)
• At [S] << Km, reaction is first-order in substrate

Title: [OC] Simulating Enzyme Kinetics with Python - The Michaelis-Menten Model

Body:

**What I built:** A Python simulation of Michaelis-Menten enzyme kinetics with parameter estimation and visualization.

**The Science (ELI5):**

Imagine enzymes as tiny machines that grab molecules (substrates) and convert them into products. The Michaelis-Menten equation describes how fast this happens:

v = (Vmax · [S]) / (Km + [S])

Think of it like a restaurant:
- Vmax = maximum orders the kitchen can handle (when fully busy)
- Km = how many customers it takes to get the kitchen half-busy
- Low Km = efficient kitchen that gets busy quickly

**What I learned:**

1. The famous hyperbolic curve emerges from simple enzyme-substrate binding kinetics
2. The Lineweaver-Burk plot (plotting 1/v vs 1/[S]) linearizes the equation, but nonlinear regression is actually more accurate
3. Parameter sensitivity matters - Km directly reflects enzyme-substrate affinity

**Code highlights:**
- Used `scipy.optimize.curve_fit` for nonlinear least squares
- Generated synthetic experimental data with Gaussian noise
- Compared true parameters vs fitted values
