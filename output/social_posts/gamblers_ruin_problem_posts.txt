TITLE: I implemented the Gambler's Ruin Problem - here's why the house ALWAYS wins (with Python code)

BODY:

**The Problem**

Imagine you have $10 and want to reach $20 by making fair 50/50 bets. What's your probability of going broke first?

**The Surprising Answer**

Even in a perfectly fair game, your probability of ruin is 1 - i/N. With $10 out of $20 total, that's 50%!

**The Math (in plain terms)**

For unfair games where you win with probability p:
- Let r = (1-p)/p
- P(ruin) = (rⁱ - rᴺ)/(1 - rᴺ)

**What I Learned**

1. **Fair doesn't mean safe**: In a fair game, the expected number of rounds is i × (N - i). You WILL eventually hit an absorbing state.

2. **The house edge is brutal**: At p = 0.49 (tiny 2% disadvantage), starting with $10 of $20, your ruin probability jumps from 50% to ~67%.

3. **Bankroll management matters**: Starting with more capital relative to your goal dramatically reduces ruin risk.

**The Code**

I ran 5000 Monte Carlo simulations for each scenario and compared them against the closed-form solutions. They match beautifully!

Key functions:
- `probability_of_ruin(i, N, p)` - analytical solution
- `simulate_gamblers_ruin(i, N, p)` - Monte Carlo validation
