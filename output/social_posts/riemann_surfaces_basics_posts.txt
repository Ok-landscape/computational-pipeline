Title: Visualizing Riemann Surfaces: How Multi-Valued Functions Get Their Own Geometry

Body:

I just finished exploring Riemann surfaces computationally and wanted to share what I learned!

**The Problem:**

Complex functions like âˆšz, log(z), and z^(1/3) are "multi-valued" - for each input z, you get multiple possible outputs. For example, âˆš4 could be +2 or -2 in the complex plane. This makes them not technically functions in the strict sense.

**The Elegant Solution:**

Riemann surfaces resolve this by creating a special topological space with multiple "sheets" where each sheet represents one branch of the function. On this surface, the function becomes genuinely single-valued!

**What I Computed:**

1. **Phase portraits**: Visualized the argument (angle) of complex functions using HSV color mapping. This reveals branch cuts beautifully - you can see discontinuities as color jumps.

2. **3D multi-sheet plots**: For w = âˆšz, I plotted both sheets (positive and negative square root) as surfaces over the z-plane. They connect at z=0, the branch point.

3. **Branch cut analysis**: Traced âˆšz along the negative real axis from above and below, showing the discontinuity jump.

4. **Monodromy computation**: Numerically verified that when you analytically continue âˆšz around a closed loop enclosing the origin, you get back -1 times your starting value (a sign flip).

5. **Elliptic curves**: Visualized wÂ² = z(z-1)(z-2), which defines a genus 1 Riemann surface with 3 branch points.

**Key Insights:**

â€¢ Branch points are where multiple sheets come together (like z=0 for âˆšz)
â€¢ The genus g is a topological invariant: g=0 for sphere, g=1 for torus, gâ‰¥2 for higher
â€¢ The Uniformization Theorem says every simply-connected Riemann surface is conformally equivalent to the sphere, plane, or unit disk
â€¢ Functions with branch point order n have monodromy factor e^(2Ï€i/n)

**Why This Matters:**

Riemann surfaces aren't just abstract math - they're fundamental to:
- Algebraic geometry (algebraic curves)
- String theory (worldsheets are Riemann surfaces)
- Integrable systems (spectral curves)
- Number theory (modular forms and elliptic curves)
