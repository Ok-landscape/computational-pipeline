Title: I implemented Clifford Algebras from scratch in Python - here's why they're fascinating

Body:

Clifford algebras (also called geometric algebras) are one of those mathematical structures that seem abstract until you realize they unify almost everything you know about rotations, complex numbers, and physics.

**What I built:**
A complete Python implementation of Cl(p,q) algebras supporting:
- Arbitrary signatures (Euclidean, Minkowski, etc.)
- Full geometric product with grade extraction
- Rotors for n-dimensional rotations
- Reflections through hyperplanes

**The key insight:**

The geometric product combines the dot and wedge products:

uv = u·v + u∧v

For orthonormal basis vectors, you get the fundamental relation eᵢeⱼ + eⱼeᵢ = 2ηᵢⱼ, meaning distinct basis vectors anticommute (eᵢeⱼ = -eⱼeᵢ).

**Why this matters:**

1. Complex numbers are just Cl(0,1) where one basis vector squares to -1
2. Quaternions are Cl(0,2) - Hamilton's i,j,k fall out naturally
3. 3D rotations use "rotors" R = exp(-Bθ/2) instead of matrices
4. Spacetime physics uses Cl(1,3) for special relativity

**The beautiful part:**

Rotations become: v' = R·v·R†

This formula works in ANY dimension, handles gimbal lock gracefully, and the rotor R = cos(θ/2) - B·sin(θ/2) directly encodes the rotation plane as a bivector B.

I verified quaternion identities (i²=j²=k²=ijk=-1) emerge automatically from the Clifford product rules.

**Interactive notebook:** https://cocalc.com/github/Ok-landscape/computational-pipeline/blob/main/notebooks/published/clifford_algebras.ipynb

The visualization shows rotation trajectories in 3D and the multiplication table of Cl(3,0).
