**Title:** Built a Python visualization system for Feynman diagrams - the pictorial language of particle physics

**Body:**

**What are Feynman diagrams?**

Imagine you want to calculate the probability that two electrons bounce off each other. In quantum field theory, this involves some seriously complex math with integrals over all possible particle paths.

In 1948, Richard Feynman invented a brilliant shortcut: draw pictures! Each line and vertex in the diagram corresponds to a specific mathematical term. Draw the picture → translate to math → get your answer.

**What I built:**

A Python visualization system that draws the 6 fundamental diagrams in Quantum Electrodynamics (QED):

1. **Basic QED vertex** - where electrons emit/absorb photons
2. **Møller scattering** - two electrons bouncing off each other
3. **Pair annihilation** - electron + positron → two photons
4. **Compton scattering** - photon bouncing off an electron
5. **Bhabha scattering** - electron + positron → electron + positron
6. **Vacuum polarization** - a quantum loop that modifies the photon propagator

**Cool physics insight:**

Also calculated how the fine structure constant α ≈ 1/137 changes with energy! At low energies (everyday physics): α⁻¹ ≈ 137. At high energies (like the Z boson mass ~91 GeV): α⁻¹ ≈ 129.

This "running" happens because virtual particle loops (like the vacuum polarization diagram) screen the electric charge differently at different scales.

**Tech stack:** Python, NumPy, Matplotlib

The wavy photon lines are generated parametrically with sine wave oscillations perpendicular to the line direction. Fermion lines use matplotlib's arrow annotations.
