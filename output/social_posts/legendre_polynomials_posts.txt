**Title:** Built a notebook exploring Legendre Polynomials - from theory to Gauss quadrature

**Body:**

I created an interactive Jupyter notebook diving deep into Legendre polynomials. Here's what I learned:

**What are Legendre polynomials?**

They're a family of orthogonal polynomials Pₙ(x) defined on [-1, 1]. "Orthogonal" means when you multiply two different ones together and integrate, you get zero - like perpendicular vectors but for functions.

**Why should you care?**

1. **Physics everywhere**: They appear in quantum mechanics, electrodynamics (multipole expansions), and anywhere you solve Laplace's equation in spherical coordinates

2. **Gauss-Legendre quadrature**: The roots of these polynomials give you the optimal points for numerical integration. With just n points, you get exact results for polynomials up to degree 2n-1!

**Key implementation insight:**

Don't use the Rodrigues formula directly (involves high-order derivatives). Use the recurrence relation instead:

(n+1)Pₙ₊₁(x) = (2n+1)xPₙ(x) - nPₙ₋₁(x)

Starting with P₀(x) = 1 and P₁(x) = x, you can compute any Pₙ efficiently.

**Cool properties I verified:**
- Pₙ(1) = 1 for all n
- Pₙ(-1) = (-1)ⁿ
- They satisfy the differential equation: d/dx[(1-x²)dPₙ/dx] + n(n+1)Pₙ = 0

The notebook includes visualizations of the polynomials, orthogonality matrix, root distributions, and Gauss quadrature convergence.
