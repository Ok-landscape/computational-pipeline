## Reddit

**Title:** I simulated small world networks and finally understand "six degrees of separation" [Python visualization + code]

**Body:**

You've probably heard of "six degrees of separation" - the idea that any two people are connected through about 6 acquaintances. But why 6? Why not 100 in a world of 8 billion people?

The answer lies in **small world networks**, formalized by Watts and Strogatz in 1998.

**ELI5 Version:**

Imagine 100 people standing in a circle, each holding hands with their 3 nearest neighbors on each side. To pass a message across the circle, it takes ~17 hops on average. Now randomly swap just 5% of those hand-holds to connect to random people across the circle.

Result? Average hops drop to ~6, but most people still have tight local friend groups.

**The Math (simplified):**

The model tracks two things:
- **Clustering** (C): How connected are your neighbors to each other? Stays at 82% of original with 5% rewiring
- **Path length** (L): Average hops between any two nodes. Drops to 38% of original with 5% rewiring

This asymmetry is the magic - clustering is local (hard to disrupt), path length is global (sensitive to shortcuts).

**What I learned:**

The visualization really drove home why this matters. Those few random long-distance connections act as "superhighways" that dramatically shrink the network while local structure stays intact. This applies to:
- Social networks (friends of friends)
- Neural networks (local circuits + long axons)
- Power grids (local redundancy + transmission lines)
