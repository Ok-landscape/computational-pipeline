TITLE: Understanding the Trapezoidal Rule - A Visual Guide to Numerical Integration

BODY:

**What is the trapezoidal rule?**

It's a method to approximate definite integrals when you can't solve them analytically. Instead of finding the exact area under a curve, you approximate it using trapezoids.

**How it works:**

Divide your interval [a, b] into n equal parts. At each point, evaluate your function. Connect consecutive points with straight lines (forming trapezoids) and sum their areas.

The formula is:
Tₙ = h × [f(a)/2 + f(x₁) + f(x₂) + ... + f(xₙ₋₁) + f(b)/2]

where h = (b-a)/n is the width of each trapezoid.

**Why is it useful?**

The error is O(n⁻²), meaning if you double the number of trapezoids, your error decreases by a factor of 4. With n=1024, I achieved errors below 10⁻⁸ for smooth functions.

**What I tested:**
- ∫₀¹ x² dx = 1/3 (polynomial)
- ∫₀π sin(x) dx = 2 (trigonometric)
- ∫₀¹ e⁻ˣ² dx ≈ 0.7468 (Gaussian)
- ∫₀¹ 1/(1+x²) dx = π/4 (rational)

All showed beautiful O(n⁻²) convergence on the log-log plots.

**The code:**

It's just ~10 lines of NumPy. The built-in `np.trapezoid()` does the same thing. My implementation matched it to machine precision.

**Limitations:**

Simpson's rule has O(n⁻⁴) convergence for smooth functions, so it's more efficient. But the trapezoidal rule is simpler to understand and implement.
