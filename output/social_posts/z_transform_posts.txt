Title: Implemented Z-Transform Analysis in Python - Pole-Zero Plots, Filter Design & Stability

---

**What is the Z-transform?**

Think of it as a "translator" that converts discrete-time signals (like audio samples) into a form where we can easily analyze frequencies and design filters. It's the discrete-time version of the Laplace transform.

**The math (simplified):**

For a signal x[n], the Z-transform is:
X(z) = sum of x[n] · z⁻ⁿ for all n

Where z is a complex number. Different values of z give you different "views" of your signal.

**Why should you care?**

1. **Filter design becomes algebra** - Instead of dealing with convolution (slow), you multiply transfer functions (fast)

2. **Stability is visual** - Plot poles on the complex plane. All inside the unit circle? Your system is stable. Simple.

3. **Frequency response** - Just evaluate your transfer function on the unit circle (z = e^(jω))

**What I implemented:**

- Numerical Z-transform computation
- Second-order Butterworth lowpass filter
- Pole-zero plot with unit circle
- Magnitude and phase response
- Impulse response
- Inverse Z-transform using partial fractions

**Key insight:**

The partial fraction method for inverse Z-transform is elegant. For X(z) = z/((z-0.5)(z-0.8)):

x[n] = (10/3)(0.8ⁿ - 0.5ⁿ)u[n]

Each pole contributes an exponential term to the time-domain signal.
