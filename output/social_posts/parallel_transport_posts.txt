Title: I built a Python simulation of parallel transport on a sphere - and proved holonomy matches curvature theory!

Body:

**What is parallel transport?**

Imagine you're standing at the North Pole holding an arrow pointing toward New York. You walk to the equator (arrow still points the same direction relative to you), walk 1/4 around the equator, then return to the pole.

Here's the weird part: your arrow is now pointing a different direction than when you started - even though you never rotated it!

**Why does this happen?**

On curved surfaces, "parallel" has no global meaning. The best we can do is keep a vector "as parallel as possible" locally, which is formalized by the covariant derivative: nabla_gamma'(t) V(t) = 0

The rotation after a closed loop is called holonomy, and it directly measures the curvature enclosed by your path.

**The math checks out**

For a spherical triangle with:
- One vertex at the pole
- Two vertices on the equator at phi = 0 and phi = pi/2

The spherical excess is exactly pi/2 (angles sum to 270 deg instead of 180 deg), and my simulation computed holonomy of 90 degrees - matching theory!

**Code highlights:**
- Used scipy.integrate.odeint for the parallel transport ODEs
- Christoffel symbols: Gamma^theta_phi_phi = -sin(theta)cos(theta), Gamma^phi_theta_phi = cot(theta)
- 3D visualization with matplotlib

This connects to Einstein's general relativity (spacetime curvature), quantum mechanics (Berry phase), and even robotics!
